North Bend Rail Trail, WV

By Attila Horvath

View North Bend Rail Trail map

View all destinations

The haunted Silver Run tunnel
There are as many variations on this ghost story as there are people telling it, so let’s just say if you encounter a transparent woman dressed in white when you go through the Silver Run tunnel (mile 21.9), stop and ask her what happened to her, so you can nail down the true story once and for all.

North Bend Rail Trail, WV photo gallery


If this 70-mile trail were near a big city, it would be famous. And crowded. And trailside amenities would be expensive. As it is, you can ride off-road on a level grade through forested mountains, pass through 10 old railroad tunnels and camp for free, all the while hoping (or not!) to encounter the ghosts that supposedly haunt this place. And yet, the North Bend Rail Trail, between Parkersburg and Clarksburg, isn’t known as an overnight bike touring destination.

“It’s cheap, fun recreation,” says D.J. Allen, owner of Country Trails Bikes in Cairo (that’s pronounced kay-ro), “You need to stop and explore.” Take D.J.’s advice and slow down to appreciate the forest, rivers, rock outcrops and the old stone and brick railroad tunnels. Expect to spot beavers and turtles, and, if you’re lucky, a river otter along the way. The North Bend is undeveloped enough to feel rugged and adventurous, but developed enough to provide amenities like campsites, a map and ice cream along the way (you would be hard pressed to spend more than $6.75 on any meal purchased trailside).

Other than a short paved portion in West Union, the trail surface is gravel and dirt and is quite rough in spots thanks to poor drainage and the horses that frequent the trail. It can also get mucky. Bring a mountain or cyclocross bike, or put knobbies on your touring bike. The western half of the trail is in better shape, so if it’s been raining heavily before your arrival, consider riding east of Ellenboro another time.

Be aware it’s not all pretty. You’ll also ride by trashed-out trailer homes and the towns you pedal through are largely decrepit. But the beauty and joy of the trail easily outweigh the ugliness, so don’t let a derelict town or two spoil the fun.

Where it’s at: Western terminus (from Parkersburg): On I-77, travel south to WV 47 east (exit 174) and go 0.2 mile to Cedar Grove Rd. Turn right and go 0.7 mile to Happy Valley Rd. Take a right and go 0.3 mile to trailhead parking on the right.

Eastern terminus (from Clarksburg): Take US 50 west 7.2 miles and turn right (north) onto Wolf Summit Rd. Make an immediate right, go 0.7 mile and turn left into the small trailhead parking area.

Cairo (midway): From US 50 (21 miles east of Parkersburg, 51 miles west of Clarksburg) turn south on WV 31. Go 4.2 miles to the trailhead located downtown.

Contact: www.northbendrailtrail.net or (304) 628-3777

Digs: The North Bend Rail Trail has 6 free primitive camps (indicated on map). North Bend State Park in Cairo offers camping ($18-$21), lodge rooms, ($59-$90) and cabins ($79-$149), www.northbendsp.com or (304) 643-2931

Grub: Trail towns have mom-and-pop restaurants (think Wonder bread and Styrofoam). The Happy Trails Café (www.happytrailscafe.com or (304) 869-3635) in Ellenboro (mile marker 34) has plenty of caffeinated beverage and some bike supplies.

Gear and Outfitting: Country Trails Bikes in Cairo has basic bike supplies like tubes and lube, (304) 628-3100. For full service bike shops, try Parkersburg Bicycle, www.parkersburgbike.com or (304) 485-BIKE; in Clarksburg try Holy Moses Cycles at (304) 622-7235 – they will arrange a shuttle.

Camping gear for bike tourists
If you plan on using the free primitive camping along the trail, make sure to bring a water filter. Only some sites have porta-potties, so bring a shit shovel and your own TP for the trip. This is in addition to your regular bike touring gear: panniers full of camping equipment, extra clothing, basic tools, extra tubes, food and lights for the tunnels and camping.
