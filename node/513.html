Marengo Cave, IN

By Mary Reed

View Marengo Cave map

View all destinations

Notice! Due to white-nose syndrome, which is killing bats throughout the East, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service has recommended a caving moratorium. Call ahead to your destination before planning your next caving trip.

Marengo Cave, IN photo gallery


When the summer and fall crowds die down, cave exploring – the headlamp-illuminated, belly-crawling kind – picks up at Marengo Cave in southern Indiana’s karst country. Here’s the deal: When fewer visitors enter the cave, different routes open up that allow you to have a purer caving experience without as many impacts on the cave or other cavers. BYOG (bring your own group, six is the minimum) and you can try some wild caving at your own speed. That means college outing clubs and Boy Scouts won’t have to adapt to each others’ paces.

“Marengo is probably the most interesting cave within 300 (miles),” says J. Pat Stephens, a director of the Louisville Grotto and former co-owner of Marengo Cave (a group of hard-core cavers bought it in the ’70s but it’s been open to the public continuously since 1883). “You’ll get wet in there because it’s actually a spring cave,” he warns, “You’ll do a lot of crawling, climbing.” Pat says Marengo is an especially pretty cave due to its many geological features: formations like the Elephant Head (yep, it looks like an elephant), crystals, bacon (not the breakfast food but calcite deposits that look remarkably like a favorite pork product), stalagmites, stalactites and gypsum flowers (again, not real flowers but just as pretty in a rocky sort of way).

Sign up for the 2-hour Underground Adventure trip; gear is provided. Along an underground stream, you’ll have a chance to see some cave-adapted wildlife including blind crayfish, blind cavefish and cave salamanders. (You’ll also have that chance to get wet.) Eastern pipistrelle bats and brown bats also call Marengo Cave home.

May through August, try the Triple Crown of Caving tour, a two-day outing that takes you to the Waterfall Crawl at Marengo Cave and the Pillar of Constitution feature (32 feet tall and 64 feet in diameter) at nearby Wyandotte Cave.

Marengo even has something for the oddity seeker: the Penny Ceiling is a room in the cave that serves as a sort of reverse wishing well. Visitors flick their change to the muddy ceiling and it sticks. In 1999, cave owners took two months to scrape, clean and sort some 90,000 coins ($3,800 worth of change). Some of the money was donated to The Nature Conservancy.

Where it’s at: About an hour from Louisville; from I-64 10 miles west of Louisville (in Indiana), take IN 64 west 25 miles to Old Main St. Take a right (north) to the Marengo Cave entrance.

Contact: www.marengocave.com, (812) 365-2705 or (888) 702-2837

Digs: Marengo Cave has a campground ($10-$25) and bunkhouse style cabins ($9/person, 4-14 capacity)

Grub: Van’s Country Table is a nearby mom and pop, (812) 365-2645

Cost: $17-$59.95
