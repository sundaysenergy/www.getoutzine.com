How to Read a Topo Map

By Mary Reed

How do you fold a map? “You accordion lengthwise and you double fold towards the center and you’re good,” says Jason Eversole of Trailqwest, who filled us in on triangulation, which is how you use a map and compass to find your location. See directions for both in photo gallery.

How to Read a Topo Map photo gallery


The more comfortable you become with reading a topographic map, the less likely you are to get lost in the woods. Here’s a primer:

Find north. If you’ve got a compass, put it on top of your topo map and align the north arrow on the map with the red end of the compass arrow. There is a difference between true north and magnetic north – the difference is called declination. “Unless you’re doing really precision work, it probably doesn’t make that much of a difference. The declination is probably within 5 to 10 degrees,” says Gerald Yip, secretary of Orienteering Cincinnati.

If you don’t have a compass and you’ve seen the sun recently, figure out east or west based on sunrise or sunset. If it’s a clear night, you can use Polaris – a.k.a. the North Star – to find north.

If the area you’re in has a major feature that dominates the landscape, orient off of that. “For example,” Gerald says, “if you’re going to a park where there is going to be a large lake on the west side of the park … then you know that as long as you keep heading toward the lake, you’ll always be heading west.”

Find your position on the map. “It’s easier to pick out where you’re standing than to pick out where you are on the map,” Gerald says. By this he means that you should view the landscape first and turn to the map second.

Whether prominent or not, there is always some kind of identifiable feature around you. Gerald suggests finding the nearest high point or nearest valley, finding an obvious human-made feature, like a road, or finding where two features cross (say, a road and a stream). Only then do you locate the feature(s) on the map.

Learn topographic symbols. The United States Geological Survey is the standard bearer of maps, so it’s good to learn USGS features:

North is always at the top edge of a USGS map, but many other maps shift north around so the map better fits on the page.

Contour lines indicate elevation and relief (slope). “A contour line means the same elevation or the same level in real life,” says Gerald. USGS uses two different scales, so be sure to check the legend to determine the elevation difference between contour lines – for our region, it’s usually 20 feet. Lines close together indicate a steep grade and lines far apart indicate a pretty flat topography. Index contours are the darker lines with the elevation marked (ex: 900 feet).

Colors on your USGS map are: brown for contour lines, blue for water, green for forest, black and red for roads, red or gray for urban areas.

Scale is a way to reduce reality to map size while maintaining the same proportions. One example: 1 inch = 2,000 feet.

Practice in controlled environments. “(Reading topo maps) is switching between the two dimensional and three dimensional,” Gerald says. At home, he suggests taking a three-dimensional object like a ball or a bunched up towel and shining a red laser pointer light along its side. The line will look like a contour line on a topo map. In the field, take a topo map to a nearby park that you know well. Practice looking at the map and the landscape to orient yourself and to get used to translating the two-dimensional map into the three-dimensional world.
