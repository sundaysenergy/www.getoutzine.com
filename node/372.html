Know Your Public Lands

By Mary Reed

There was that time my friend and I brought her dog to a state nature preserve only to leave it tied to a tree in the parking lot – didn’t know dogs weren’t allowed (oops). Then there were my friends who went backpacking in a state forest only to discover a huge clearcut where the trail used to be. Bummer, to say the least. And then there were those coworkers who wanted to visit that nearby national park. Only problem was, there was no nearby national park.

Erin Haddix St. John, a West Virginia field rep for the National Parks Conservation Association, points out that it’s not only good for us to know the regulations of the lands we’re visiting in order to comply with the law, but it’s good to be plugged in with land administrators in order to maintain or open access for our particular sports. “(Instead of going) straight to the climbing area or getting right on your bike, take some extra time to know what kind of public land you’re on,” Erin says. “Who manages it and knowing what their goals and challenges are … is what allows public land managers and the recreation community to work together well.”

Here’s a rundown of what’s out there, public land-wise, so you can better prepare for your next trip and know how to advocate for both your sport and the place itself. But know that even this information is generalized; be sure to check in with the agency that administers the public land you’re about to visit.

NATIONAL PARKS SYSTEM
Examples: Mammoth Cave National Park, KY, New River Gorge National River, WV
Administered by: National Park Service
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, but often with restrictions. For example, in some parks, mountain biking may not be permitted on trails.
Hunting permitted? No in national parks; yes in some units such as national recreation areas
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state fishing regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Yes, usually
Pets allowed? Yes, on a leash in designated areas
Logging and mining permitted? No
Foraging permitted? Generally prohibited, usually only allowed for research purposes

NATIONAL FORESTS
Examples: Allegheny, PA; Wayne, OH; Monongahela, WV; Daniel Boone, KY; Hoosier, IN; Shawnee, IL
Administered by: U.S. Forest Service
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, restrictions may apply in wilderness areas
Hunting permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Yes
Pets allowed? Yes, on a leash
Logging and mining permitted? Yes, except in wilderness areas
Foraging allowed? Yes, with a permit

STATE PARKS
Examples: Hocking Hills State Park, OH; Ohiopyle State Park, PA; Brown County State Park, IN
Administered by: Each state
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, but types vary from park to park
Hunting permitted? Varies, check with the state park before you visit
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Yes
Pets allowed? Yes, on a leash
Logging and mineral extraction permitted? Generally no, but there are some exceptions
Foraging permitted? Varies

STATE FORESTS
Examples: Hocking State Forest, OH; Kanawha State Forest, WV
Administered by: Each state
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, but restrictions vary greatly
Hunting permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Generally yes
Pets allowed? Yes, on a leash
Logging and mining permitted? Yes, some areas have restrictions
Foraging permitted? Generally yes, with a permit

STATE WILDLIFE AREAS
Examples: Atterbury Fish and Wildlife Area, IN; Hughes River Wildlife Management Area, WV
Administered by: Each state
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Generally yes, but some lands are leased
Recreation permitted? Yes, but these lands are generally set up for hunting (for example, there may be no official trails)
Hunting permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Generally only where there are facilities such as a campground
Pets allowed? Varies
Logging and mining permitted? Generally no

STATE NATURE PRESERVES
Examples: Cumberland Falls State Park Nature Preserve, KY; Hueston Woods State Nature Preserve, OH (these two preserves are within state parks)
Administered by: Each state
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, but usually restricted to only hiking, birdwatching and photography or boating if there's a river or lake
Hunting permitted? Generally no
Fishing permitted? Generally yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Generally no
Pets allowed? Generally no
Logging and mining permitted? Generally no
Foraging permitted? Generally no

METROPARKS
Examples: Pittsburgh Parks Conservancy, Columbus Metroparks, Five Rivers MetroParks (Dayton), Cincinnati Parks Department, Louisville Metro Parks, Indy Parks
Administered by: Each city
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Yes
Recreation permitted? Yes, but each park varies in its restrictions
Hunting permitted? Varies, usually prohibited or restricted
Fishing permitted? Yes, in accordance with state regulations
Overnight stays permitted? Varies, but most do not allow overnight stays
Pets allowed? Yes, on a leash
Logging and mining permitted? Generally no

PRIVATE AND COMMERCIAL LANDS
Examples: Snowshoe Mountain, WV; Wisp Resort, MD; lands owned by The Nature Conservancy and Audubon
Administered by: Property owner
Saved for the public in perpetuity? Varies, but these lands often have some sort of conservation easement preserving them
Recreation permitted? Yes, usually
Hunting permitted? Varies
Fishing permitted? Varies
Overnight stays permitted? Varies
Pets allowed? Varies (this is getting annoying, isn’t it?)
Logging and mining permitted? Varies
Foraging permitted? Varies
