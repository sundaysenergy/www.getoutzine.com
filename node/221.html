Going the Distance

The dos and don'ts of endurance training

By Alison Wayner

Whether you’re a participant or spectator, here are some 2008 endurance events in the Ohio River region

Louisville Ironman

Tour of the Scioto River Valley (OH)

Cincinnati Flying Pig Marathon

Brown County Breakdown (IN)

Rachel Carson Trail Challenge, Pittsburgh

24 Hours of Big Bear (WV)

I nearly bonked during my 16-miler today: I was hungry (only ate half a granola bar before I started), dehydrated (one too many Blue Moons last night – hey, it was Saturday), and tired (consequence of said Blue Moons). I never realized how hard training for a marathon would be until I tried. As a former collegiate track and cross country runner, I figured jumping up to 26.2 miles would be a piece of cake. I was wrong.

Chris Schwirian, an instructor in Ohio University's Biological Sciences Department who specializes in human and exercise physiology, notes that taking on endurance training – whether it be for a marathon, century ride, triathlon or extended hiking or backpacking excursion – means taking on a change in lifestyle.

It takes time
Training properly for an endurance event will take several months – don't sign up for an event that's just around the corner. And schedule the number of hours weekly it will take to properly train – it may be more than you think.

Get a checkup
Schwirian suggests having an initial checkup by a physician and letting them know about your upcoming training is probably enough (unless you are at risk for heart and other cardiovascular diseases).

Equip yourself
Always purchase the appropriate footwear and training equipment. This helps prevent injuries and makes the activity more enjoyable.

Don’t overdo it
The first step in endurance training, Schwirian says, is to not overdo it. "Too many people think they need to go hard everyday or go long every day and that's how they end up sidelined with an injury," he says. "A general rule of thumb is to never increase your training volume by more than 10 percent per week."

Eat enough calories
An increase in appetite is a normal physiological response to increases in exercise intensity and distance. The more miles you're logging, the more fuel your body needs to sustain that training. For those who are endurance training in order to lose weight, Schwirian says it's fine to gradually cut calories, but be mindful of eating regular, well-balanced meals and drinking plenty of water. "Never start serious endurance training while slashing calories. You're using more energy and your muscle fibers are getting bigger – you need more food to keep this up."

Hydrate
Consume water before, and sports drinks during and after your bout of exercise to replace lost electrolytes. Also, don't wait until you're thirsty to begin hydrating – by that time, your body's already dehydrated. Hydrate continuously throughout the day, as an individual involved in endurance training needs more liquids than the average Joe/Jane. Remember that liquids containing caffeine and alcohol are diuretics that cause dehydration and should be consumed moderately.

Rest up
Allowing your body to rest allows it to repair itself from the stresses of training. Schwirian says it's best to consistently follow hard training days with one or two easy days consisting of light activity. Taking one or two days a week completely off won't hurt your training and may actually help stave off injuries.

Don't do it alone
It's always best to have someone to train with – not only for safety, but also for sanity.

Do something
Even if you don't get to do as much of a workout as you planned, it’s always better to do something than nothing. On days when you really don't feel like training, throw on your gear and head out the door for 10 to 15 minutes. Nine times out of 10, you'll keep going.

Ali Wayner lives in Athens, Ohio, and is training for the May 4 Eugene (OR) Marathon. She is foregoing Blue Moon until (immediately) after the race.
