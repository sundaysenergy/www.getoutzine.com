Protect Your Skin from the Sun

By Mary Reed

I was born with both an insatiable appetite for the outdoors and some of the whitest skin you’ve ever seen. But still, it was somewhat shocking when I had a precancerous lesion removed from my face in my early 30s. That was all it took to make me get serious about protecting my skin from the sun.

“You can be an outdoorsy person, but if you’re smart about it – appropriate sunscreen, sun protective clothing, (going outside) certain times of the day when the sun’s not beating down on you, you can have certain risk but less risk than someone who goes to the tanning bed,” says Lawrence Mark, assistant professor of dermatology at the Indiana University School of Medicine.

My own practices that I’ve developed closely mirror Mark’s recommendations, and they apply to people of all skin tones:

Avoidance. If I can get outside in the morning or evening instead of mid-afternoon, I do it. But there’s nothing I love better than being outside all day, so my avoidance techniques sometimes need to get a little more refined. For example, I’m a rock climber, so I’ll check the guidebook to find climbs that are in the shade during different parts of the day and I’ll follow the shade accordingly.

Coverage. I hate sunscreen because it’s loaded with chemicals that I don’t really know anything about and it makes my skin feel greasy. So before hitting the bottle, I put on a hat and a long-sleeve shirt. When I have plenty of space, I wear this nifty broad, metal-rimmed hat from ifactoryoutlet. If I’m carrying a backpack or otherwise don’t have all that shoulder room for a wide-brimmed hat, I wear a short-brimmed hat or a billed cap (baseball cap or visor).

Sunscreen. Although I’m not a fan of it, I do wear sunscreen regularly – I need it even when I’m wearing a hat if I’m in the sun for hours. Consumer Reports gave Neutrogena Ultra Sheer Dry-Touch SPF 45 the overall highest score among the sunscreens it tested. It gave No-Ad Sunblock Lotion Maximum SPF 45 its Best Buy designation.

But what about those chemicals? The Environmental Working Group has created a list of sunscreens that it tested for both effectiveness and potential health hazards. It has eight sunscreens that it rates both effective and low-hazard. View the list here.

“SPF only speaks to UVB protection,” Mark says, pointing out that our skin needs protection from UVA – ultraviolet A – radiation as well. You might need to do a little extra research before buying your next bottle of sunscreen to see if it has UVA protection. In addition, Mark says, using sunscreen doesn’t negate the first two tactics for protecting your skin from the sun. “Sunscreen reduces the amount of UV that gets to the skin, but it doesn’t block all of it, so you still have to think about good protection practices, like avoiding the sun midday.”

Practicing these good habits regularly – don’t slip up and earn yourself a second-degree sunburn – should ensure a long, skin-healthy life of outdoor enjoyment. And if all else fails, there’s always caving.
