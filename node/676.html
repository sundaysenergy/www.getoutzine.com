Laurel Ridge State Park, PA

By Lisa Kaufman

View cross-country ski trail map

View all destinations

If you have a hankering for the snow-bunny crowd after all the peace and quiet, Seven Springs Mountain Resort and Hidden Valley Four Seasons Resort are off the same turnpike exit.

Laurel Ridge State Park, PA photo gallery


If you yearn for that quiet spot you can ski to in the woods that hasn't been trampled to bare ground by a mad crush of downhill powder bunnies, southwestern PA's Laurel Ridge deserves a red circle on your map of places to check out this winter. Add a couple stars for no-fuss on-site rentals, two warming huts and occasional free lessons from friendly volunteers.

Nestled in the southern section of the 13,625-acre park astride Laurel Mountain, a cross-country concession rents equipment and grooms 20 miles of trails through the woods. The public-private enterprise is a hybrid in more than one sense, catering to classical cross-country skiers (for whom tracks are groomed in the snow) and skate skiers (those competitive types who need compacted snow). "It's probably the only (non-private) ski venue in western Pennsylvania that offers skating and classical skiing, because we groom the trails for both styles. That makes us unique, and people come from all over," says Rick Garstka, president of the Pennsylvania Cross Country Skiers' Association.

PACCSA works with the state parks department to improve and maintain trails and operates a webcam (www.paccsa.org/webcam.htm) updated hourly so you can check out conditions before making the drive. The site also lists the schedule of free lessons – conditions permitting. "Even though it's a good snow-catch area, the weather is so unpredictable and variable that we can't really rely on consistent conditions," Rick says.

The trails are color-coded, and concessionaire Bob Ruppel of Laurel Ridge State Park Cross Country Ski Center says the 3.2-mile Red Trail is definitely the favored one. “(It) has the best base, gets the most attention, and it is an intermediate trail with several good downhills and some sharp turns."

Rick (the flat Garstka Loop was named after him) says the Yellow Trail is the most challenging. "It's got a long downhill and a long uphill, and coming back is just a grind." He says it’s for the “backcountry bush whackers” as are some trails off of the Yellow that are ungroomed where the more adventurous can cut their own tracks.

There's a trail fee when the concession is open; call first if you don't have your own gear and are depending on the rentals. When the concession is closed, trail use is free and one warming hut is unlocked so you can build yourself a cozy fire in the woodstove.

The surrounding park, whose centerpiece is the 70-mile all-seasons Laurel Highlands Trail, has about 35 miles of skiable trails, and overnight Adirondack-style shelters for camping.

Where it's at: About 1 ½ hours southeast of Pittsburgh; take the PA Turnpike to Exit 91 (Donegal); turn left onto Route 31 east, then right onto PA 711 south, and go 10 miles to Normalville. At Normalville, turn left and go about 0.1 mile, then another left onto PA 653 east. After about 5.5 miles, turn right at the sign for the Laurel Ridge Cross Country Ski Center.

Contact: Laurel Ridge State Park or (724) 455-3744 or (814) 445-7725 in winter

Digs: The Winter Cabins at Kooser State Park have two miles of trails out the door, ($75 and up), www.koosercabins.com or (412) 720-4074; Log Cabin Motel & Suites ($79 and up) www.logcabinmotel.com; 800-753-8105 or (724) 593-8200

Grub: Brady's Restaurant in Acme is also off the Donegal exit, www.bradysrestaurant.com or (724) 423-4566

Gear and Outfitting: Laurel Ridge Cross Country Ski Center, (724) 455-7303; classical rentals are $15 adults/$7 juniors (ages 6-12); skating rentals are $26/$23

Cost: Trail use is $6 adults/$3 juniors when concession is open; no charge when concession is closed, but trail use is still permitted
