Muskingum River, OH

By Mary Reed, photos by Attila Horvath

View all destinations

View Muskingum River Water Trail map (pdf)

Fridays through Mondays from Memorial Day through Labor Day, lock technicians are on hand to lock you through from 9:30 a.m. to 6 p.m. You might be able to get a special lock through if you call ahead two weeks. Lock technicians take lunch from 2:00 to 2:30. Same hours apply on weekends May through mid-October.

Muskingum River, OH photo gallery


Put your boat in Ohio’s Muskingum River and paddle back in time. As you travel down this state-designated water trail, you’ll have the chance to paddle through the country’s only remaining hand-operated locks, which date back to 1841.

“Basically, it’s gravity and manpower,” says Earl Campfield, a lock technician at the Devola Lock and Dam, as he puts his weight into an iron handle that engages a rack and pinion system that opens and closes the wooden doors of the sandstone lock. “It’s really like taking a ride back through history, (it’s) something they did 100 years ago.”

Cast your eyes upward and you’ll see bald eagles and great blue herons, just like 100 years ago. Bring your fishing rod to troll for catfish and bass.

If you’re feeling ambitious, paddle the length of the 112-mile river – putting in north of Coshocton and taking out at Marietta, which sits at the confluence of the Muskingum and Ohio Rivers. An ambitious and fit paddler can take on this flatwater trip in three days; a week to 10 days would be more doable for most.

Not only will you experience the locks and dams – a National Historic Civil Engineering Landmark and a National Park Service Navigation Historic District – you’ll travel through remote natural areas, and find yourself near prehistoric Native American earthworks and Underground Railroad sites.

Between the primitive camping alongside the locks (for through-boaters only) and private campgrounds, you shouldn’t go farther than 27 miles between riverside digs – most distances are considerably shorter.

For a day trip, try the nine miles from McConnellsville to Stockport, home to a mill that was originally built in 1842 and is now the only remaining mill on the Muskingum. For the 50 miles from McConnellsville to the river’s mouth in Marietta, campsites are between three and 10 miles apart, making kayak/canoe camping trips very doable.

Where it’s at: An hour east of Columbus; take I-70 east about 55 miles to Zanesville where you will reach the Muskingum River and OH 60. Take route 60 north or south to your desired put in.

Contact: www.ohiodnr.com/parks/muskngmr or (740) 453-4377

Digs: Free primitive camping (for through-boaters only) is available at locks 8 through 2; several private campgrounds are riverside, find their info on the Muskingum River Water Trail pdf; the Stockport Mill in Stockport has riverside rooms ($69-$299), www.stockportmill.com or (740) 559-2822; the Lafayette Hotel in Marietta is around the bend from the Muskingum upstream on the bank of the Ohio, ($65-$175) www.lafayettehotel.com or (740) 373-5522 or 1-800-331-9336

Grub: Within walking distance of the river are Zak’s Restaurant in Zanesville, (740) 453-2227; the Stockport Mill, www.stockportmill.com/restaurant or (740) 559-2822; Brighter Day in Marietta, (740) 374-2429

Brew: Marietta Brewing Company, www.mariettabrewingcompany.com or (740) 373-BREW

Gear and Outfitting: Southeastern Ohio Kayaking, http://s.o.kayaking.tripod.com or (740) 767-3780

Cost: $5 fee to lock through once, $15 weekend and $50 seasonal lock passes are also available

Preparing for Your Trip
If you want to take a long-distance trip on the Muskingum, you’ll need to look for buoys upstream of lowhead dams. You cannot paddle over the dams on the Muskingum. In some places you can’t portage around the locks and dams either. To let the lock technician know you want to lock through, you have two options. Option 1 is the old fashioned way: bring a whistle and blow one long and one short whistle that the lock technician can hear. Option 2 is the newfangled way: bring a cell phone and call before arriving at the lock – all locks have phone numbers. View phone numbers on page 1 of the Muskingum River Water Trail pdf.

If you’re planning to camp, bring all of your own camping gear and food, plus a water purifier. Bring a volleyball if you want to enjoy some of the maintained sandy campside courts.
